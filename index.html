Manuel Blum


    Manuel Blum was:
  • A Mathematician
  • A Computer Scientist

Through his axiomatic approach to computational complexity theory in the 1960's, Manuel Blum gave us theorems such as the compression theorem, the gap theorem, and the Blum speedup theorem.

Year Event
1938 Manuel Blum was born on April 26, in Caracas, Venezuela.
1959-1961 Blum earned a Bachelor degree and a master's degree in electrical engineering from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.
1964 Blum earned a doctorate in mathematics from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. After his studies, he joined the computer science department at the University of California.
1988 Blum is elected to the American Association for the Advancement of Science.
1995 Blum wins the A.M. Turing Award, the highest honour in computer science, for his contributions to the foundations of computational complexity theory and its application to cryptography and program checking.
1999 Carnegie Mellon University succeeds in recruiting Blum and his wife, Lenore.
2000 Yahoo! Inc, an American Internet search engine company, contacts Carnegie Mellon's computer science department for help with distinguishing computer and human visitors to its website. This led to Blum and other scientists creating CAPTCHA(completely automated public Turing test to tell computers and humans apart).
2002 Blum is elected to the U.S. National Academy of Sciences.
2006 Blum is elected to the U.S. National Academy of Engineering.

You are all computer scientists. You know what FINITE AUTOMATA can do. You know what TURING MACHINES can do. For example, Finite Automata can add but not multiply. Turing Machines can compute any computable function. Turing machines are incredibly more powerful than Finite Automata. Yet the only difference between a FA and a TM is that the TM, unlike the FA, has paper and pencil. Think about it. It tells you something about the power of writing. Without writing, you are reduced to a finite automaton. With writing you have the extraordinary power of a Turing machine.

-Manuel Blum
